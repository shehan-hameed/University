Here are solid answers for each:

"What sensors are used in relation to the idea?"

There are five sensor types used in the system:

  • DHT22 — measures temperature and humidity inside the classroom
  • MQ135 — measures CO₂ levels and air quality
  • BH1750 — measures ambient light level
  • Sound sensor — measures noise level
  • MQ2 — detects smoke and fire

Together these five give a complete picture of the classroom environment and safety status.

"Why are they used?"

Each one maps directly to a feature:

  • DHT22 + MQ135 → trigger the AC and fan via relay when the room gets hot or stuffy
  • BH1750 → controls the lights based on how bright or dark it is
  • Sound sensor → detects if the classroom is too noisy, feeds the environment model
  • MQ2 → triggers the emergency evacuation headcount when smoke or fire is detected

None of them are there "just because" — each one drives a specific automated action.

"Why not use other sensors instead?"

This is the one they'll push hardest on. Here's how to answer each:

DHT22 vs DHT11: DHT11 is cheaper (around Rs. 400) but only accurate to ±2°C. DHT22 gives ±0.5°C accuracy which matters when you're making automated decisions about AC control. A 2 degree error could mean the AC never turns on or never turns off.

MQ135 vs MQ7 (carbon monoxide only): MQ7 only detects CO. MQ135 detects CO₂, ammonia, benzene and general air quality — much more relevant for a crowded classroom environment where CO₂ buildup from breathing is the main concern.

BH1750 vs LDR (light dependent resistor): An LDR gives a raw analog resistance reading — you'd have to calibrate it yourself and it drifts over time. BH1750 gives a direct digital lux reading over I²C, much more accurate and consistent, and barely costs more.

MQ2 vs a dedicated smoke detector: A standalone smoke detector just beeps — it can't send a signal to the ESP32-S3. MQ2 gives a digital output directly to the microcontroller so the system can automatically trigger the evacuation headcount dashboard. That's the whole point.

Sound sensor vs a dedicated decibel meter: A proper SPL meter costs Rs. 5,000+. The sound sensor module at Rs. 330 is enough to detect relative noise levels for classroom management — we don't need precise decibel measurements, just a threshold trigger.

Memorise the DHT22 vs DHT11 and BH1750 vs LDR comparisons especially — those are the most likely follow-ups.
